Suppose we are playing a game of tag. Let $ P $ be the pursuer and $ E $ be the evader, and say we have selected a payoff/win condition (cf. {{<ref "Win Conditions" "/notes/win-conditions">}}) of: $ P $ wins if they capture $ E $ in under a minute, and $ E $ wins otherwise. We then have the following question: how can $ P $ "pursue optimally" in order to win? (We mean "optimally" in the sense that $ P $ captures $ E $ in under a minute the highest possible proportion of the time.) Some possible options would be:
1. note where $ E $ is and their speed in each instant, and extrapolate their position (say in a straightline) and try to intersect with them; or
2. run directly after $ E $ and hopefully just catch up to them.{{%nl%}}
But, as Isaacs puts it, "A brief reflection shows that such questions are meaningless". Indeed, he is right as everything we suggested as strategies in our example, all were based on $ E $'s movement (either during an instant or extrapolate from many instants). From this we arrive at this powerful and poignant idea that Isaacs gifts us: **"We cannot talk of optimal pursuit without also speaking of optimal evasion"**. {{%nl%}}{{%nl%}}

Indeed, we can only develop optimal stratagems for, say, the pursuer if we simultaneously consider the optimal stratagems for the evader. I found this idea super cool and very enlightening (but I say that not knowing much of anything yet...).
