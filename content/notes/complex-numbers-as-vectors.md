Recall the [definition of $ \mathbb{C} $]({{<ref "/notes/definition-of-the-complex-numbers">}}). Recognizing that, in addition, the real and imaginary part of the complex number are "independent" of one another (i.e., we cannot simply $ x+iy $ into a purely real or purely imaginary complex number if $ x,y \neq 0 $), it becomes clear that we may identify complex numbers with elements of $ \mathbb{R}^2 $! Indeed, we can use the following map: 
$$
    f \colon \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{R}^2 \quad \text{defined by} \quad x+iy \mapsto (x,y).
$$
(Notice that this map is invertible, so our identification goes both ways.){{%nl%}}{{%nl%}}

Identifying them with vectors goes further into both of their addition properties. Indeed, addition in the complex numbers quite handily corresponds to tip-to-tail addition of vectors in $ \mathbb{R}^2 $. We will expand upon this more in the post [geometry of complex arithmetic]({{<ref "/notes/geometry-of-complex-arithmetic">}}).
