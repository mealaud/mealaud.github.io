Recall the definition of $ \mathbb{C} $ from {{<ref "this post" "/notes/definition-of-the-complex-numbers">}}. We may now recognize that, in the addition of complex numbers, the real and imaginary parts "independent" of one another (i.e., we cannot simply $ x+iy $ into a purely real or purely imaginary complex number if $ x,y \neq 0 $). Hence, we may then identify complex numbers with elements of $ \mathbb{R}^2 $! Indeed, we can use the following map: 
$$
    f \colon \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{R}^2 \quad \text{defined by} \quad x+iy \mapsto (x,y).
$$
(Notice that this map is invertible, so our identification goes both ways.){{%nl%}}{{%nl%}}

Identifying them with vectors goes further into both of their addition properties. Indeed, addition in the complex numbers quite handily corresponds to tip-to-tail addition of vectors in $ \mathbb{R}^2 $. We will expand upon this more in the post {{<ref "Geometry of Complex Arithmetic" "/notes/geometry-of-complex-arithmetic">}}.
