Topology & Continuity III (Connectedness) Monday, September 16, 2024

Let’s motivate first. You may or may not remember the Intermediate Value Theorem from calculus: given a continuous function $ f \colon [a,b] \to \mathbb{R} $ then for all $ f(a) \lesseqgtr y \lesseqgtr f(b) $1, there is some $ c \in [a,b] $ with $ f(c) = y $. Note that when we understand the function to be continuous, our domain is exactly what gives us this property.

Indeed, consider the function $ f \colon [0,1] \cup [2,3] \to \mathbb{R} $ given by $ f(x)=x $ and consider the value $ 1.5 $. Here we attain $ f(0)=0 $ and $ f(3)=3 $ with $ 1.5 \in [0,3] $, but DO NOT attain $ 1.5 $. Why?

This failure comes $ [a,b] $ have no “holes” or “gaps” whereas $ [0,1] \cup [2,3] $ has a “hole” or “gap” from $ (1,2) $. If there was no gap in our domain, we would have reached our desired value. In this sense, we have to have a connected domain.

Connectedness is kind of hard to define directly. We know for sure when something is not connected (or is disconnected) but the positive answer is not nearly as obvious. For example, which of following are connected?

  1. Is $ A \cup B $ connected?
  1. Is this curve connected?
  1. Is the large blob $ A \cup B $ connected?

It seems that the first is connected. The second is entirely unclear (at least it was to me at first). We know that the third example is certainly not connected.

So what characterizes these? Well, in the sense of open sets, we can see that (3) has open sets that totally separate the blobs $ A $ and $ B $. And it seems as if we cannot do that in (1). (I will save (2) for later.)

It turns out that this notion of separation can be made more rigorous with the following definition: given a set $ A $, it is disconnected if there are a pair of disjoint open sets whose union is $ A $. From here, we get that a set is connected if it is not disconnected.
