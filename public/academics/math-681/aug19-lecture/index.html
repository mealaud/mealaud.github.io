August 20th Lecture Tuesday, August 20, 2024

We started with a brief, high-level overview of what topology is. Topology is simply “geometry made flexible, without rigid notions of distance”. This general-seeming idea grew out of the study of surfaces in complex analysis and algebraic geometry.

Topological spaces

A topological space is a set $ X $ together with a collection $ \mathcal{T} $ of subsets $ X $ such that

  1. $ \varnothing, X \in \mathcal{T} $,
  2. any union of elements of $ \mathcal{T} $ is in $ \mathcal{T} $, and
  3. any finite intersection of elements of $ \mathcal{T} $ is in $ \mathcal{T} $. The collection of subsets $ \mathcal{T} $ is known as a topology . In most cases, we’ll drop the $ \mathcal{T} $ and just write “$ X $ is a topological space”.

The structure of a topology is ubiquitous in mathematics and so we have a lot of examples. We name a few for the time being:

  1. The standard topology on $ \mathbb{R} $ (if every point of a set contains an open interval contained in the set, then the set is open)
  2. If $ X $ is any set, $ \mathcal{P}(X) $ forms a topology on $ X $. We call this the discrete topology .
  3. If $ X $ is any set, we can take $ \mathcal{T} = \left \{ \varnothing, X \right \} $ to get the indiscrete topology .
